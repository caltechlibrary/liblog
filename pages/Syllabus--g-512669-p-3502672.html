Medieval Knighthood

Syllabus

This course will introduce students to the knight, i.e., the heavily armed and armored warrior on horseback who perhaps even more than monks or Gothic cathedrals represents the European Middle Ages in the popular consciousness. We will tell the knight's story from his beginnings in the early Middle Ages, through his zenith in the eleventh, twelfth and thirteenth centuries, to his decline and transformation in the late medieval and early modern periods. The course will treat the knight not simply as a military phenomenon (although there will be plenty of discussion of medieval military tactics and technology), but also as a social, political, religious, and cultural figure who personified many of the elements that set the Middle Ages apart.

Readings : The readings for this course blend primary sources with secondary scholarship on knights. The required books are:

1. Carolingian Chronicles: Royal Frankish Annals and Nithard's Histories , trans. Bernhard Walter Scholz

2. The Book of Sainte Foy , trans. Pamela Sheingorn

3. Ottonian Germany: The Chronicon of Thietmar of Merseburg , trans. David A. Warner.

4. Fulcher of Chartres, A History of the Expedition to Jerusalem, 1095-1127 , ed. Harold S. Fink, trans. Frances Rita Ryan.

5. Galbert of Bruges, The Murder of Charles the Good , trans. James Bruce Ross.

6. Georges Duby, William Marshall: The Flower of Chivalry .

7. The Song of the Cathar Wars: A History of the Albigensian Crusade .

8. Ulrich von Liechtenstein, The Service of Ladies , trans. J. W. Thomas.

9. Geoffroi de Charny, A Knight's Own Book of Chivalry , trans. Elspeth Kennedy.

I will supplement these books with texts that I will post as handouts on the course website at various points in the course.

Grading : The course focuses on the analysis and discussion of the readings. Attendance and class participation will therefore make up 40% of your grade. There will be no midterm and no final examination. Instead, I will ask you to write three papers on issues arising from the readings at select points during the course. These papers will together make up the remaining 60% of your grade.

In addition, I will ask you to write short summaries/reaction papers before each discussion meeting. These papers MAY BE NO LONGER THAN one page long. They should include a BRIEF summary of the essential points or essential content of the reading(s) and a BRIEF statement of your reaction, i.e., things you found interesting, questions you had, things you agreed/disagreed with, etc. These papers will help me set up and guide the discussions. Please send these papers to me VIA E-MAIL. They must reach my in-box BY 10.00 AM ON THE DAY OF THE RELEVANT DISCUSSION. In accordance with their purpose, I will not grade these papers as I would essays; instead, they will form part of your attendance/participation grade.

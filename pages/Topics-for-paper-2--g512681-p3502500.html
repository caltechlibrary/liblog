TOPICS FOR PAPER II

Answer one of the following questions in a two to three thousand word (or more, if you feel like it), double spaced paper:

1. What sort of hero image does Galbert project in the Murder of Charles the Good ? In other words, who is (are) his hero (es), and what makes him (them) heroic? Who is (are) his villain (s) and what makes him (them) villainous? How do these images fit with what you know about early twelfth century Flanders in particular and early twelfth century European society in general?

2. Read Anselm of Canterbury’s Proslogion (in the Geary Reader starting on p. 329). Compare his proof of the existence of God with that of Thomas Aquinas (in the excerpt from the Summa Theologica that we discussed in class). How are they the same; how are they different? From your comparison, can you say anything about how scholastic thought in Europe developed between the eleventh and the thirteenth centuries?

3. By the time Jean, Sire de Joinville, wrote his Life of St. Louis (ca. 1307-1371), the French King Louis IX had already been canonized a saint (1297). According to Joinville, what was it exactly that made King Louis a saint? How does this image of sainthood compare with the image Joinville projects of the Church?

4. In the Enquêts of St. Louis, the royal bailli Girard de La Boiste appears with some frequency. From his appearances (as well of those of any other interesting figures you can find), build a picture of his activities and the things he did to make himself unpopular. Does it appear that the sort of royal investigation that produced the Enquêts was necessary?

5. Both Froissart’s Chronicles and Galbert of Bruges’ Murder of Charles the Good deal with knights and knightly violence. Compare the violence and knightly (or aristocratic) behavior visible in the two works. Do you see similarities? Differences? Some combination of the two? Can you make an argument from your conclusions about the development over time of ethics or cultural norms concerning the use of violence?

[Hint: to properly answer this question, you will need to deal with the authors, their sources, and their perspectives/agendas].

[Note: you could also choose to do this with Froissart and the Cid].

6. Explain to me, in detail and with examples, how the medieval English exchequer worked and how it was used. Not the office, but rather the calculating board itself. If you can place it in a historical continuum of devices (i.e., tell me where the English got it), so much the better. Please cite your sources.

TOPICS FOR PAPER I

Answer one of the following questions in a two to three thousand word, double-spaced, paper, drawing on material from the texts concerned:

1. The Passion of SS. Perpetua and Felicity and St. Augustine's On Christian Doctrine are profoundly different kinds of texts. Is it possible, however, to argue that they in fact share a great deal in common? If so, what things do they share, and how do they each express them? Finally, if they do connect on some level, can you explain why they are so different from each other?

2. Assume for the moment that Augustine’s On Christian Doctrine serves as a blueprint for a Christian society. What do you think that society might look like, and why?

3. Although Jordanes' History of the Goths and the Hildebrandlied mention some of the same people, they describe the barbarian peoples they deal with very differently. Nevertheless: are there any resonances between them? Put differently, are there elements of Jordanes' narrative that match the image of barbarian society projected by the Hildebrandlied ? What does a comparison of the two texts tell us about how useful they are as sources of information about the barbarian peoples?

4. We can describe the Hildebrandslied as a barbarian view of sixth-century barbarians, though from a considerable remove (i.e., the early ninth-century, mediated through centuries of oral tradition). Jordanes is from the sixth century itself, but his view is that of a Romanized barbarian. Moving further back in time, we find a fully Roman view of Germanic barbarians in the Germania , written by Cornelius Tacitus towards the end of the first century. Read the Germania ; it is in the Geary reader and it is not long. Then compare it to Jordanes and to the Hildebrandslied , and tell me what you find. Does Tacitus resonate at all with Jordanes and/or the Hildebrandslied , and if so how? Does it help us penetrate through the difficulties with these sources to grasp something of the barbarian society beyond them?

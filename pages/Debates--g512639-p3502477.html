Debate: Church v. Empire

Introduction :

On Monday and Wednesday, May 11 and 13, we will hold formal debates in class to settle once and for all the question of the proper relationship between religion and government, or, more specifically, between the head of the Roman church and the secular leader (s) of western Christendom. The debate will be informed by the historical conflict for political and religious supremacy between the Roman Popes and the Roman (i.e., German) Emperors that began in the late eleventh century with the Investiture Controversy and continued throughout the Middle Ages.

To give everyone in the class a chance to weigh in on this momentous issue, I have divided the class into two groups. Each group will hold its own debate on one of the two debating days. Within each group, I have formed three teams. Two of these teams will carry out the debate. The third team will form the judging panel. This panel will have the awesome responsibility of deciding which of the two debating teams has won, and for explaining exactly why.

Weaponry:

The core resources for the debate will consist of documents from the actual medieval controversy, in which the principals to the conflict or partisan intellectuals marshaled evidence and presented arguments in favor of one side or another. These documents consist of:

1. the assigned readings in the Geary Reader for Week VII: 1) Canons of the Fourth Lateran Council; 2) Pope Gregory VII and King Henry IV - The Investiture Controversy; 3) Concordat of Worms; 4) Otto of Freising, The Deeds of Frederick Barbarossa.

2. Documents on the WWW at the Internet Medieval Sourcebook .  From the home page, go to " Selected Sources " -->" Empire and Papacy "; there you will find primary sources not in Geary. In addition, if you get ambitious and want to go back a step farther into the primary source material, go to "Full Text Sources," where you will find complete e-text editions of the writings of the Latin Church Fathers, and the acts of various church councils.

3. Documents contained in Brian Tierney, The Crisis of Church and State 1050-1300 . I have placed this on reserve in the Dabney Library.

4. The Bible. I have placed an English translation of the Latin Vulgate Bible on reserve in the Dabney Library. If you have an edition of your own you would prefer to use, feel free.

I have also placed three books on reserve which will give you further background should you need or want it, and possibly some help in shaping your arguments:

1. Kishlansky, Geary, and O'Brien, eds., Civilization in the West - "The Universal States: Empire and Papacy" gives a general overview paralleling my lectures.

2. Geoffrey Barraclough, The Medieval Papacy - chapters III and IV provide more extensive background.

3. Ute-Renate Blumenthal, The Investiture Controversy - an entire (short) book on the subject.

If you find that another team has checked them out and hidden them, come see me; I have personal copies you may use (this applies also to the Bible and to the Tierney above).

Debate Plan:

The actual thesis under debate: The authority of the Bishop of Rome is supreme, not only over the Christian Church, but also over that of any lay ruler. On each of the two days, Team I will argue for this thesis; Team II will argue against it.

The debate will proceed as follows: each team will make an opening presentation introducing and stating its thesis. Each will then make a formal presentation of its argument, i.e., the reasons why its thesis is true and the evidence that supports it. The opposing team will have a chance to rebut the argument, followed by a counter-rebuttal from the first team. The second team will then make its argument, followed by a rebuttal and counter-rebuttal. Each team will close with a concluding statement wrapping up its arguments in light of what has emerged during the debate and hopefully dealing a final crushing blow to its opponent.

The formal structure:

Team I Opening Statement - 3 min.

Team II Opening Statement - 3 min.

Team I Formal Argument - 3 min.

Team II Rebuttal - 2 min.

Team I Counter-Rebuttal - 2 min.

Team II Formal Argument - 3 min.

Team I Rebuttal - 2 min.

Team II Counter- Rebuttal - 2 min.

Team I Concluding Statement - 3 min.

Team II Concluding Statement - 3 min.

After a short (5 min.) break, we will reconvene, and the panel of judges will have an opportunity to cross-examine the teams. One judge will ask Team I a question and a member of Team I will have 2 min. to respond; another judge will ask Team II a question and a member of Team II will have 2 min. to respond. If the judges deem it necessary, they may repeat this cycle one more time.

After the cross-examination is complete, the panel of judges will withdraw and quickly (within 5 min.) prepare a statement (again ca. 3 min.) declaring who has won, why, and how the respective arguments stacked up against the panel's view of the matter.

Hints:

I am assuming that each debating team will divide up the five stages of the debate and its response(s) to the judges’ questions among its members as it sees fit. Pinch-hitting is discouraged; I want to hear everyone speak . As you can see, you will need to prepare in advance for the opening statements and the formal arguments. You can try to predict what your opponent will say, and thus sketch out rebuttals and counter-rebuttals and concluding statements, but you will have in the final analysis to improvise these on the spot in response to what your opponents actually say. The same, of course, holds true for the cross-examination period.

The panels of judges have the hardest job, and I will be watching them the most closely . The judges will collectively have to master both sides of the debate in order to properly evaluate what each team tries to argue. You will also have to be able to think on your collective feet, in case one or both teams come up with anything unorthodox. As the debate unfolds, you will have to take notes and confer (quietly) in order to prepare questions for cross-examination that properly destabilize both teams. Your verdict and supporting statement will be a collaborative effort, drawn up on the spot at the end of the debate. You will have to select in advance who will ask the questions and who will read the verdict and statement (I would prefer that they all be different people). I will therefore evaluate you on the quality of your collective effort as manifested in your questions and evaluation of the two teams.

If any of the groups would like to meet with me at any stage for help, feel free.

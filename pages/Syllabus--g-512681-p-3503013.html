The High and Late Middle Ages

Syllabus

This course is designed to introduce you to the history of Europe between 1000 and 1400. It will survey the economic, social, political, and religious evolution of the region during this period, with a focus on France, England, Italy and Germany.

Readings : the readings for this course consist of primary sources. Three books are required. The first two are: The Poem of the Cid , and Jean Froissart, Chronicles . The third is a collection of sources that we will dip in and out of throughout the quarter: Patrick J. Geary ed., Readings in Medieval History (referred to below as Geary, with the name of specific selections). In addition to these three books, you will also occasionally get readings in the form of handouts.

Course requirements and grading : The course is built on the reading, analysis, and discussion of the material listed above. Class attendance and participation will therefore make up 20% of your grade. Before each discussion meeting, I will ask you to a write short summary/reaction paper on that day’s reading. These papers MAY BE NO LONGER THAN one page long. They should include a BRIEF summary of the essential points or essential content of the reading(s) and a BRIEF statement of your reaction, i.e., things you found interesting, questions you had, things you agreed/disagreed with, etc. These papers will help me set up and guide the discussions. Please send these papers to me VIA E-MAIL. They must reach my in-box BY 10.00 AM ON THE DAY OF THE RELEVANT DISCUSSION. In accordance with their purpose, I will not grade these papers as I would essays; instead, they will form part of your attendance/participation grade.

In addition, in lieu of a midterm, you will write two papers of at least two thousand words each on one or more of the readings. I will provide paper topics in advance. If you have questions of your own that you would like to pursue, however, you may do so; please clear your topics with me. The two papers together will make up 40% of your grade. A final examination, making up 30% of your grade, will end the course.

In the seventh week we will hold a formal debate in class, to decide once and for all the proper relationship between religion and government and in particular between Church and Empire. Your preparation and participation in this debate will make up the remaining 10% of your course grade.

Website : the course has a website, whose URL is given above. A copy of this syllabus is on the site. All supplementary materials for the course will be posted to the site, that is, reading handouts, paper topics, and the debate rules and resources. The site also contains links to other resources for the study of the Middle Ages, as well as useful guides and tools for writing essays both for this class and elsewhere.

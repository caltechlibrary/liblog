The Crusades

Syllabus

This course will introduce you to the series of religiously motivated European invasions of the Middle and Near East that began at the end of the eleventh century and that led to the creation of Latin Christian principalities in Palestine. Though the crusading movement came to embroil much of Europe itself, the course will focus strictly on the military expeditions to what the Crusaders called the Holy Land, and the history of the Crusader states up to the point of their destruction at the end of the thirteenth century.

The course will be guided by the following questions: how did medieval Christianity justify wars of aggression against foreign peoples and religions? What motivated western Europeans to leave their homes and march into a hostile environment, where they often faced impoverishment if not death and where maintaining a Christian presence was a constant struggle? How did the Crusaders manage to erect stable political entities in alien territory that lasted as long as they did, and how did they have to adapt their own culture to do so? Finally, how did the native peoples of the regions the Crusaders invaded and conquered – Muslim but also Christian and Jewish – perceive the Crusaders? How did the Crusaders’ presence affect life in a region whose populations had their own ancient histories and patterns of life?

Readings : The readings for the course concentrate on primary sources. The following books are required:

1. S. J. Allen and E. Amt, eds., The Crusades: A Reader ( given below as Reader)

2. F. Gabrieli, ed., Arab Historians of the Crusades (given below as Historians)

3. Anna Komnene, P. Frankopan and E. R. A. Sewter, eds. and trans, The Alexiad

4. M. R. B. Shaw, ed. and trans., Joinville and Villehardoin: Chronicles of the Crusades

In addition, we will be reading materials that I will post to the course website or provide to you as handouts. These readings are given in the course schedule below.

Grading : The course focuses on the reading, analysis, and discussion of the material listed above. Class participation and attendance will therefore make up 40% of your grade. There will be no midterm and no final examination. Instead, I will ask you to write three papers on issues concerning the Crusades at select points during the course. These papers will together make up the remaining 60% of your grade.

In addition, I will ask you to write short summaries/reaction papers before each discussion meeting. These papers MAY BE NO LONGER THAN one page long. They should include a BRIEF summary of the essential points or essential content of the reading(s) and a BRIEF statement of your reaction, i.e., things you found interesting, questions you had, things you agreed/disagreed with, etc. These papers will help me set up and guide the discussions. Please send these papers to me VIA E-MAIL. They must reach my in-box BY 10.00 AM ON THE DAY OF THE RELEVANT DISCUSSION. In accordance with their purpose, I will not grade these papers as I would essays; instead, they will form part of your attendance/participation grade.

Late Paper Policy

In general, I do not give extensions on papers unless an emergency or other serious problem prevents you from getting a paper in on time. If such a problem arises, please see me and we’ll work something out. In all other cases, I will apply penalties to late papers according to the following schedule:

• one to three days late: one half letter grade off.

• later than that: a whole letter grade off.

Drafts :

Since it is part of my job to help you learn to write better, I will be happy to look at drafts of your essays before you hand them in, and give you comments and suggestions. I just need warning and time enough to do so before the last minute, i.e., so that I have time to read them and so that you have time to revise them before they are due. The first two papers this term are due on a Wednesday (Feb. 1) and a Friday (Feb. 24) respectively; I will therefore take a look at drafts if they are given to me no later than Friday Jan. 27 and Tuesday Feb. 21 respectively so that I can have them back to you in time for you to revise them before they are due. The third paper is due at the end of finals week, Friday, March 16; I will accept drafts if they are given to me no later than Tuesday March 13 .

II. The 9 th and 10 th centuries

-aristocracy of Charlemagne’s time (ca. 800) not “legal” nobility but rather identified by wealth and office: aristocrat had a great deal of land, had offices, either in the Church (bishop, archbishop, abbot of a monastery) or secular ( count : person placed by king in a specific geographical area w/duties of administering justice and commanding local troop levy).

-aristocrats who were not in Church regarded warfare as crucial component of lifestyle (and also some that were, but will talk about that later). Trained from childhood to fight. Maintained own groups of followers and fought in or commanded parts of king’s army. Warfare main pursuit – Charlemagne always fighting someone every year – and war was a major source of income, both in plunder and in land.

-horses important to warfare in 9 th c., but not in way would become later. Great magnates prided themselves on skill in horseback warfare, maintained mounted followings, but horses mainly useful for getting places. Most Frankish warfare done on foot; cavalry used for what it usually used for: scouting, raiding, flank attacks, etc.

[Carolingian Empire]

-Charlemagne and successors had chosen office holders from large pool. Could be Frankish families from home area of Carolingian family (Austrasia: now Belgium/NW Germany), or local families whose loyalty bought by rewards of royal service.

-no legal heredity of office; Charlemagne and successors might allow count’s son to succeed him, but didn’t have to. In order to remain loyal, though, great families expected at least some office in every generation.

-consequence: family consciousness focused around officeholders whose offices might change from generation to generation and who might be in different branches of family – your kinship identity could be drawn from either paternal or maternal lines depending on which prestigious person you wanted to identify with à horizontal or “ bilateral ” concept of kinship.

-example: if your father from middling aristocratic family, married woman from top family, lots of land, villas, maybe her brother was archbishop – you would want to identify yourself w/her kindred. Can see in naming patterns: people drawing names or pieces of names from mother’s side.

-moreover: although identifiable “regional” aristocracies in different parts of Empire, all were interconnected by blood and marriage; individual aristocratic kindreds could have lands or hold offices (sometimes several at a time) spread all across Europe.

[Europe 843]

-civil wars among Charlemagne's grandsons, competition among successors forced members of "imperial aristocracy" to choose among alternative kings, to identify themselves w/regional kingdoms rather than w/supra-regional empire.

-as 9c. turned into 10c., situation made worse in W. Frankish kingdom (France) because nowhere for monarchy to expand. Expansion had helped hold Charlemagne’s kingdom together – constant inflow of plunder, lands, offices to hand out. In 10c. France, much more profitable for aristocrats to intrigue against or fight each other in pursuit of land and power, or attach themselves and their ambitions to competing claimants for kingship in hope of rewards and offices.

-competition among Charlemagne’s descendants turned into competition among leading aristocratic families – Carolingians became simply one competitor among many. In France led to replacement of the Carolingians by the Capetian dynasty; in the East by Saxon Ottonians.

-Fierce bidding for support among competitors for crown in West enabled magnates to sell military/political support to highest bidder. Big part of bargaining: heredity of office - same office passed down in same line of family as a right.

-heredity of office tends to focus your family’s interests on particular area: where your office is.

[10c. invasions]

-something else that tended localize power, from mid. 9c. on: external invasion. From E. Magyars/Hungarians; from S: Arab raiders from Spain; from N. Vikings from Scandinavia.

-as mentioned before: kind of warfare these groups practiced, each in own way, very difficult for traditional Frankish army to handle. Vikings liked to come up rivers; could come and be gone before army could be assembled.

-situation made worse by fact that particularly in France hard to build united front against them. Political infighting in 10c. France very intense - someone always ready to ally selves w/Vikings to help them in fight against someone else. By end of 9c., some Vikings able to maintain themselves in France for years, wintering in fortified bases. Some ended up settling permanently.

-character of military situation in W. by turn of 10c. placed premium on local or regional defense. Most successful aristocrats those who could defend (or control) local areas the best (ancestors of Capetian kings of France, Roberting family, were in late 9c. Counts of Paris - got big boost to prestige from ability to defend Paris from Vikings). Aristocratic reputations to be made or broken in localities/regions.

-Let’s look closer now at who was doing fighting. Aristocratic warleaders need followers to be warleaders. Followers at top end of scale could themselves be free aristocrats w/their own followings attaching themselves to someone more powerful. Or, at bottom, coiuld be serfs: unfree people owned by aristocrats and supported as soldiers.

-all kinds of people did all kinds of fighting w/all kinds of weapons. In unsettled situation of 10c. really important fighters were ones w/horses. Horses very useful in this kind of environment for couple of reasons: can move quickly and can carry people w/lots of armor on. Enable quick short-range response to from fortified base to raid (by invader or neighbor), or staging of own raid w/quick return to base.

-military value of horse enhanced by technological improvement: somewhere in here (900-1100) - adoption of stirrup together w/high-backed saddle gave heavily armed mounted warrior ability to use lance in new way: mounted shock charge w/couched lance (explain).

[Henry IV v. Henry V battle] – Germany c. 1100

-horses expensive to buy and keep; only wealthiest (read, most successful) could afford to fight from horses, provide horses for followers. Therefore: if you were fighting from horseback, were either an aristocrat or attached to one.

-talk briefly about fortified bases. Forts of course nothing new. Romans had had them, Roman cities had been walled, Charlemagne understood how to build forts and capture them.

-Starting in 10c. France, start to see small forts, made for handful of men (dozens) at most. Called literally “forts” – Latin “ castel ” – “ castle .” Not yet big stone structures out of movies. Wooden structures, built on hilltops to command/dominate countryside, provide knights w/fortified base for rapid response, local raiding. If no hill available, you built one - " motte " (Fr. for hill).

[Model of Motte and Bailey]

-what was innovative about castles was size. Small, designed to hold small garrison, not defend town population or entire army. Cheap, easy to put up. Since small, built high - easy for small group of men to defend.

-11c. period of " encastellation " of Europe - castles sprang up everywhere. According to archeology, hard to find castles of this sort prior to millenium.

-So: hereditary offices, geographically located, and focus on local and regional military success based on castles: encourages vertical family consciousness, rooted in heir to family office and lands. Ca. 1000 family names or surnames emerge among aristocracy, bound to location. "Somebody from Somewhere," "somewhere" frequently being a castle (von, van, de).

-important to avoid endangering family position by dividing inheritance - > " patrilineage " - passing property, office, and family consciousness vertically through male line. Had developed first among kings, in course of 10c. spreads to aristocracy.

-inheritance in course of 10-11c. tends to get focused in eldest son. Daughters given dowries, married off, younger sons sent to church or given use rights to family property (but not ownership). Often only elder son marries - younger sons not allowed to reproduce legitimately unless they can provide (marry into, conquer) own patrimony. Called " primogeniture ."

III. devolution of power

- in course of 10c., loss of royal control extends outwards. Counts lose control of " Viscounts ", in some places viscounts lose control of castellans (people in immediate control of castles).

-what we might call "public" functions (taxation, justice, military command) – perhaps better to call them “royal” possessions - become personal and heritable possessions. In some regions of France, particularly south, political and economic power becomes in final analysis possession of castellans w/mounted thugs in castles, who control castle and territory immediately around it.

-change in terminology used to describe situation at turn of millennium: rather than government, historians speak of "lordship", based on appearance of lat. dominus = lord at lower and lower social levels. Lordship meant ability to dominate, ultimately based on military power. At lowest level, castellans and other lower lords dominate peasantry militarily and through private courts.

-Courts only as effective as lord's military power - only those people show up that lord can force to, or who feel that courts serve their interests. Symptomatic of change in nature of power in France that in late 10c. great aristocrats stop showing up at king’s courts.

-this could ebb and flow: strong leaders could impose will on castellans, create larger lordships that for awhile (generation or two?) looked like successful political entities – could spring up out of nowhere, could vanish as quickly as they came.

III. Glue holding society togther: Kinship, Loyalty, Interest.

-is all chaos in areas of France where this went the farthest? No. Some mechanisms allowing society to function year in/year out, generation to generation

-1) kinship is a stabilizing force. Family networks formed safety net for individuals, resource for aid if necessary. Example: kin had ancient obligation to revenge murder. If someone killed in a feud, ancient laws required compensation to his family. If payment not made, lurking in the background was threat of revenge, and escalating violence. Threat of revenge force for order.

-2) vassalage/oaths of fealty. Warleaders built coalitions of warriors by engaging their respect and self-interest. Military success, brought followers eager to cash in on plunder and land, associate their families w/a successful one.

-warriors swore fidelity to lord, promised to show up at courts, give military service, in exchange got protection, and property to support military service. Called " vassal " - from lat. vassus , originally meaning servant. Property grant called " fief ". Fiefs can include estates, tolls, courts w/income etc. Origin of word "fee".

[Etymology of “fief”: Old French “fiu” from Old High German “fehu” = cattle, property, money -> wages?]

-combination of pledge of loyalty w/property grant in exchange for service: later called feudalism , from lat. foedus = treaty or alliance. "Feudalism" hot topic right now. Problem infinite variablity in arrangements: fidelity w/out fief, fief w/out sworn oath, oaths to more than one lord, fiefs from more than one lord. Some want to scrap label altogether as a later imposition of a systematic concept on a much richer medieval reality, others want to talk about different "feudalisms".

-Me: tend to find term still useful, because it relates to fundamental way power worked around year 1000: land or other property in exchange for service. Arrangements anchored in visible set of norms (visible in narratives, poems, later in laws) about mutual loyalty between lord and vassal (even if honored more in breach than in practice).

-effectiveness of vassalage tied up w/self-interest: how much is my alliance to this lord worth to me? In theory, Dukes and Counts hold offices and at least some of their lands from king - his vassals. In practice, allegiance didn't mean much, not much king could give them.

-ties into comments about what happens to landless younger sons in society where eldest son usually got family property and office. Could take military training and go on market as freelance warriors. Aim to build reputation as fighters in someone's service, and hope to get a fief from someone more powerful -basis on which to start a family, and start line of their own, attract own landless youths as followers -->become great aristocrats themselves.

V. Social consequences of changes in warfare

-as I said before, decentralization of power most extreme in central and southern France. Also happened to greater or lesser extent in N. Italy, Christian parts of N. Spain. No accident that in these regions starting in 11c., a common bond or common culture began to connect all who rode a horse in armor w/lance or sword and shield - bond that gradually transcended actual status– because these were ones who wielded political power.

-can see cultural change in language. Latin term for soldier: "miles"/milites, originally meant simple soldier. In 10c. began to gradually change meaning, from simple soldier, often unfree, to mounted soldier (miles) as opposed to footsoldier (pedites). That is, soldiers who counted. From late 10c./11c. and onwards, that historians start to translate "miles" as "knight" in sense that we understand it now -- a member of a developing nobility characterized not necessarily by wealth but by practice of mounted warfare.

-stress that French phenomenon; different in Germany. There decentralization not so radical, mounted soldier/followers frequently serfs and remained that way. Knights remained distinct from upper aristocracy into the 13c. Will talk about this later in course.

-in France/England, "miles" became term that not only described military function but also aristocratic lifestyle; by 12c. even kings would call themselves knights.

V. Norman Conquest of England

[map w/Normandy/England]

-end by talking about Norman conquest of England 1066 as symptomatic of changes. Normans descended from Vikings settled around mouth of Seine early 10c. (hence Normandy – Land of Northmen). Aggressively adopted French style of warfare; line of capable and aggressive Norman dukes developed one of most successful and long-lasting principalities in W.

-England at time ruled by line of Anglo-Saxon kings (in 1066 Edward the Confessor ), governing mixed Saxon-Danish aristocracy (Danish part coming from Vikings who had come to England starting in 9c.)

-Duke William of Normandy ("the conqueror", or at the time , “the bastard”) based his claim to English throne on kinship to Edward through mother, alleged promise by leading Saxon/Danish claimant, Earl Harold Godwinson , to respect his rights. Got mad when King Edward made Harold his successor, even madder when Harold accepted. Invaded.

-Norman invasion of England, in particular events leading up to pivotal Battle of Hastings 1066, captured shortly after fact (from Norman point of view) in Bayeux Tapestry - woven representation of events commissioned by Odo, Bishop of Bayeux in Normandy (William’s half-brother)

-clash of two military/aristocratic cultures: Saxon-Danish army fought on foot; Normans fought from horseback. Neither inherently superior - was true and would remain true for centuries that disciplined body of well-armed and armored infantry in good position could defeat horsemen. Two cultures had evolved under two different sets of conditions.

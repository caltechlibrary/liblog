TOPICS FOR PAPER II

Answer one of the following two questions in a paper of at least 2000 words, double-spaced, drawing on material from the primary sources concerned.

1. Re-read the Salic Law carefully. Drawing on your reading of its preoccupations, sketch a picture of society in the early Merovingian kingdom. Do you think this picture is accurate? Why or why not? (note: if you would like to read the entire Salic Law before answering this question let me know and I will give it to you).

2. In our discussion of the Salic Law, we bumped repeatedly into the question of what the text was for, how to make the individual clauses work together, how a judge might have applied them, etc. What we did not do (though I had wanted to) was to compare what we saw in the Salic Law with references to “law” in Gregory of Tours. Now is your chance to do so. Compare our readings from the Salic Law with Gregory of Tours’ account of the Austregisel/Sichar/Chramnesind affair (Books VII and IX). Does reading the latter help you understand the former? Or are the images of law projected by the two sources completely irreconcilable?

3. We have read two texts from the late Merovingian period: the Life of St. Balthild and the last eleven chapters from the Book of the History of the Franks. Compare the images of Merovingian royal and aristocratic life that the two texts present; do they complement or contradict each other? If the former, how? If the latter, how can they come from the same time and region?

4. The introductory paragraph to Einhard's Life of Charlemagne in the Geary reader suggests that the Life is "an extremely subtle critique of Charlemagne's successor," i.e., Louis the Pious. Supposing for the moment that this is true, how does Einhard criticize Louis and what form does his criticism take? Finally, based on your reading of the text, do you accept the argument that the Life is in fact a criticism of Louis?

And for those who want to read even farther ahead and tackle this before we discuss it…

5. Liudprand of Cremona's description of imperial/papal relations in the mid-tenth century has entertained generations of students and scholars. Some have gone so far as to use him as evidence for a papal "pornocracy" under Pope John XII. My question is: do you believe Liudprand? If so, why? If not, why not? If you do not, why do you think Liudprand described events the way he did?

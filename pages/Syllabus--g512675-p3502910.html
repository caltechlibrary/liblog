The Early Middle Ages

Syllabus

This course will introduce you to the formative period of western medieval history, roughly from the fourth through the tenth centuries. It will concentrate on the development of a new European civilization from the fusion of Roman, Germanic, and Christian traditions, with a focus on the Frankish world. The course emphasizes the reading, analysis, and discussion of primary sources.

Readings : Each week, we will read and discuss specific primary sources from Patrick J. Geary, ed., Readings in Medieval History (given in the schedule below as Readings , with the name of specific selections). This book is therefore required; you must bring it to class discussions. I will occasionally augment the Geary reader with additional readings, which I will hand out in class.

Course requirements and grading : The course focuses on the reading, analysis, and discussion of the required readings. Attendance and class participation will therefore make up 30% of your grade. Before each discussion meeting, I will ask you to write short summaries/reaction papers. These papers MAY BE NO LONGER THAN one page long. They should include a BRIEF summary of the essential points or essential content of the reading(s) and a BRIEF statement of your reaction, i.e., things you found interesting, questions you had, things you agreed/disagreed with, etc. These papers will help me set up and guide the discussions. Please send these papers to me VIA E-MAIL. They must reach my in-box BY 10.00 AM ON THE DAY OF THE RELEVANT DISCUSSION. In accordance with their purpose, I will not grade these papers as I would essays; instead, they will form part of your attendance/participation grade.

In addition, in lieu of a midterm, you will write two papers of from 2 to 3 thousand words each (ca. seven to ten double-spaced pages) on one or more of the readings. I will provide paper topics in advance. If you have questions of your own that you would like to pursue, however, you may do so; please clear your topics with me. The two papers will make up 50% of your grade. A final examination, making up the remaining 20%, will end the course.

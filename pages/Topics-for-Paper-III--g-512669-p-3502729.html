TOPICS FOR PAPER III

Answer one of the following questions in a paper of no less than 2000 words, drawing on material from both the relevant readings and the lectures as you think necessary:

1. Georges Duby goes out of his way to portray the twelfth century as the period when the chivalric society and culture represented by William Marshall was at its height. We have read two sources that bracket Marshall’s lifetime: just before his birth, Galbert of Bruges’ Murder of Charles the Good , and roughly contemporary with his death, The Song of the Cathar Wars . How do the images of the good knight projected by Duby compare with those that you can derive from Galbert and Cathar Wars ? Are they the same or different? What do you make of the comparison?

2. Look back across all of the readings for the course. How do you understand the ongoing and evolving role played by the Christian religion in chivalric society/culture? I am interested in particular in how you think the relationship between the Christian component of chivalry and the Christian church developed over time. Do you think Christian chivalry was a product of the church, a religious calling in its own right that maintained its independence from the church, or some combination of the two?

3. What role do women play in the knight’s world, as you have seen it evolve over time?

4. Compare Ulrich of Liechtenstein’s Service of Ladies to other sources we have read from the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries (i.e. William Marshall, Song of the Cathar Wars, Geoffrey de Charny; you may include the optional excerpts from Jean Froissart if you would like). Does Ulrich’s poem bear any relationship to reality as depicted by these other sources?

5. We started the course with an image of the medieval knight as a creature who combined military with social, political, cultural, and religious elements; he was a heavily armed and armored warrior who fought from horseback with lance, sword and shield, who was noble and wielded political power, who followed a particular set of moral and ethical rules incorporating loyalty, service, justice, and mercy, and who regarded his profession as a religious calling. After working your way through the readings for this course, do you still think this is true? Is this what the knight was, or became?
